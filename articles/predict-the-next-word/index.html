Why “predict the next word” shouldn’t work this well (but does)

Mar 30, 2026 · originally published on Medium

LLMs are still just predicting the next word.

Two years ago, that was reason enough to dismiss them — it explained their awkward output. Then they got dramatically better, and scale alone doesn’t explain why.

If you’re new to LLMs, you should watch Karpathy’s “Deep Dive into LLMs” and read Lee & Trott’s “LLMs Explained with a Minimum of Math and Jargon”.

The 2024 mental model of LLMs

You probably already know LLMs are stateless next-token predictors trained on lots of text that store information in a weird compressed form.

Internally, it’s a very complex pattern-matching machine that produces tokens.

If you asked ChatGPT in 2024:

Q: Is it cheaper to fly from London to Barcelona or take the train next Friday?

It would give you a mostly useless answer:

A: Flying is generally cheaper than taking the train for this route.
Budget airlines like Ryanair and easyJet often offer flights from London to Barcelona for around £30–£80 if booked in advance. The flight takes about 2 hours.
By contrast, taking the train usually involves a Eurostar from London to Paris, followed by a high-speed train to Barcelona. This can cost between £120–£200 and takes around 10–12 hours in total.
So flying is likely the cheaper and faster option, while the train may be more comfortable and environmentally friendly.

Even though nothing fundamental changed since 2024, we’ll see that the 2026 answer is in a completely different league.

Models learning to say “I don’t know”

In 2024, one of the most frustrating things about LLMs was that they’d confidently hallucinate.

The simple solution was to teach models to say “I don’t know” — and then give them something to do about it. (This sounds pretty obvious in hindsight)

They’re trained to recognise patterns that signal uncertainty — like being asked for real-time data — and respond with a refusal. Like this:

Q: What is the weather in Paris?
A: Sorry, I don’t have access to live weather data. You could check weather.com.

Models are taught humility by example.

This turns out to achieve much more than just not being annoying. Once a model can recognise when it lacks information, it can also learn to do something about it.

Asking for data (tool use)

Instead of refusing, models can be trained to ask for help — by generating a tool call that the system intercepts, executes, and feeds back as context.

Going back to our weather example. You ask:

Q: What is the weather in Paris?
→ [TOOL_CALL: web_search(“weather Paris”)]
→ (result injected into context)
A: It’s currently 18°C and partly cloudy in Paris.

From the user’s perspective, this is all behind the scenes. After you ask a question, you might see a “Searching the web…” indicator, and you get your answer.

But from the model’s perspective, nothing fundamental changed. It’s still just generating tokens. The trick is that the environment now reacts to certain tokens by fetching real data.

Instead of relying on its internal compressed knowledge (which is frozen at training time), the model has access to the entire internet, proprietary databases, or any API linked to the system. If it needs to perform some calculations, it can just write a small Python program and ask the host to execute it.

Reasoning

In 2024, models typically generated answers in a single pass.

But that’s not required. The human brains that produced the training data don’t work that way. We think through problems, challenge our assumptions, and sometimes spend years on a question.

The first hint that LLMs could do something similar came in 2022, when researchers showed that simply prompting a model to ‘think step by step’ improved its performance on reasoning tasks. This technique is called “chain-of-thought prompting”, but the model is still working alone — nobody’s checking its steps.

Automating thinking

For this technique to become useful for real-life research problems, the human can ask “think step by step and ask for feedback after every step,” and then guide the conversation towards the desired result.

The obvious next step is to remove the human from the loop. Instead of the user guiding the model step by step, another model does it — or the same model plays both roles, challenging its own answers.

This could work. But something more interesting happened when researchers used “Reinforcement Learning”(RL).

Reinforcement learning

A base model (after pretraining) is given a bunch of tasks, run in a loop. Each time, it responds differently, and each response is evaluated. Over many iterations, through trial and error, the model gets better at producing responses that score well, because those get reinforced in the weights.

This means the model’s internal pattern matching becomes more precise — the next time it encounters a similar problem, the processes that led to the correct answer activate more strongly, making the model more likely to follow that successful approach.

The raw information was already there in the base model. RL only makes it overwhelmingly more likely for the model to use it properly. It uses computation to improve the structure; it injects meta-information into the system.

This is the first way to trade computation for intelligence.

Note: Early LLMs used RL with human raters judging which responses were ‘better’ (RLHF). This made models polite but was subjective and expensive. The real breakthrough was RLVR — reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards, using tasks such as maths and code, where correctness can be checked automatically. This scales because verification is much easier than solving.

Native LLM Reasoning

After RL with Verifiable Rewards(RLVR), something unexpected happened: models started producing reasoning-like steps. Nobody told them to break problems into steps — the weights shifted that way because methodical approaches produce more correct answers.

Remember the idea of two models challenging each other? This is that, but happening inside a single model during a single response.

When the generated text looks like a complicated problem, the model writes ‘Let me break this down.’ When it finishes a sub-problem, it writes ‘Let me double-check.’ When it hits a dead end: ‘This doesn’t look right, let me reconsider.’

This is far stronger than prompted chain-of-thought, because the model has learned to recognise patterns of difficulty that no prompt engineer would think to list.

RL is a result-oriented process. Reasoning was an outcome.

Inference-Time Compute and Reasoning

Once a model has learned to reason through RL, you can make it smarter in a surprisingly simple way: let it think longer.

At inference — the moment you actually ask the question — the model can generate more tokens, explore alternatives, and backtrack before committing to an answer. Without the RL training, more tokens would most likely be noise, but with training, the model favours patterns associated with productive thinking.

LLM Reasoning is searching over possible solution paths, guided by learned heuristics.

This is the second form of the trade. Intelligence is no longer just what’s baked into the weights during training — it’s also how much computation you’re willing to spend at the moment you ask the question.

When it all comes together

These capabilities work well together. A model that can reason and use tools is fundamentally different from one that can do either alone.

Let’s go back to the question from the start:

Q: Is it cheaper to fly from London to Barcelona or take the train, if I’m travelling next Friday?

We saw the kind of useless answer the 2024 model would give us.

A model with tool use alone might search the web and return some results, but struggle to compare them sensibly. A model with reasoning alone might lay out a brilliant analytical framework with made-up numbers.

But a model with both capabilities can reason about what data it needs, make multiple tool calls to look up flight and train prices, write a small program to compare the options accounting for factors like travel time and airport transfers, get the result from the program, recognise when a search result looks outdated, try again — and then present you with a well-reasoned answer grounded in real data.

The reasoning drives the tool use, and the tool use grounds the reasoning.

Conclusion

LLMs are still just predicting the next word.

But you can teach a next-word predictor to admit what it doesn’t know. You can give it access to tools. You can train it to pause and think before answering, or to challenge its own conclusions.

The result is genuinely useful in practice — even though models still hallucinate sometimes, overthink and overengineer, and get stuck in loops - and the jump from 2024 is enormous.

That’s the part I didn’t see coming.
